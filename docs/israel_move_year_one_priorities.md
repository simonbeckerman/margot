# Israel Move – Initial Priorities (Year One)

**Last updated:** 24 April 2026

## Purpose

This document governs the first year of Simon and Chiara’s relocation from the UK to Israel.

Its role is to anchor decisions around stability, belonging, health, relationships, and practical integration.

When decisions feel noisy, rushed, overly technical, or overly optimised, this document should act as the human and strategic compass.

This document should be read together with the Israel Move – Source of Truth and the supporting advisor documents.

This document is intentionally stable. It should not be used to track live dates, day counts, deadlines, or operational admin.

---

## 1. What This Move Is

This move is:

- A long-horizon relocation from the UK to Israel.
- A re-anchoring of life around meaning, people, and sustainability.
- A chance to build a real home base in Israel.
- A family and life decision, not only a tax decision.
- A gradual process of integration, not a forced reinvention.

---

## 2. What This Move Is Not

This move is not:

- A tactical tax migration only.
- A productivity sprint.
- A forced business restart.
- A reason to over-structure life or assets too early.
- A reason to ignore emotional stability, health, or daily sustainability.

---

## 3. Time Horizon

Think in years, not weeks.

The first year is explicitly for:

- Acclimatisation.
- Emotional and physical stability.
- Building a stable home.
- Language acquisition.
- Creating daily routines.
- Building belonging.
- Forming real friendships and community.
- Understanding Israel’s rhythm, culture, and professional ecosystem.

The first year is not for:

- Forcing outcomes.
- Rushing business creation.
- Over-optimising taxes or structures at the expense of stability.
- Making irreversible decisions before daily life is understood.

---

## 4. Decision Hierarchy

When inputs conflict, decisions should be made in this order:

1. Chiara’s wellbeing and family emotional safety.
2. Presence and daily sustainability.
3. Health, calm, and stability.
4. People, relationships, and community.
5. Legal and tax robustness.
6. Efficiency and optimisation.

If something is technically efficient but emotionally draining early on, it is probably wrong for year one.

---

## 5. Guiding Principles

- Presence over speed.
- Depth over breadth.
- Robustness over elegance.
- Sequencing over cleverness.
- Fewer structures, but ones that survive stress.
- Reversibility preferred.
- Avoid premature decisions.
- Pressure undermines sincerity.

This applies equally to life, religion, finance, work, and community.

---

## 6. Core Priorities for Year One

The priorities below are intentionally ordered.

### 1. Settling In

Create a stable home base in Israel.

This includes:

- Completing the move from London.
- Setting up the Tel Aviv home.
- Furnishing the apartment thoughtfully but without unnecessary pressure.
- Understanding the neighbourhood and daily logistics.
- Building routines around food, sleep, health, work, and rest.

The goal is to feel grounded, not rushed.

### 2. Chiara’s Wellbeing

Chiara’s wellbeing is central.

The first year should reduce pressure, not increase it.

Important considerations:

- Emotional calm.
- Health and physical comfort.
- Social belonging.
- A sense of agency in the move.
- Space to decide what Israel feels like in real life.

No plan is successful if it works technically but feels unsustainable for Chiara.

### 3. Learning Hebrew

Hebrew is infrastructure for life in Israel.

During the first six months, language learning should be treated as a major priority, not a side project.

The goal is practical integration:

- Daily conversation.
- Reading signs, messages, and basic documents.
- Feeling less dependent on English.
- Building confidence in ordinary life.

### 4. Building Community

Jewish and Israeli community should be experienced from the inside, through ordinary life and relationships.

The priority is:

- Finding people who feel natural.
- Exploring shuls and communities without pressure.
- Building real friendships.
- Participating in Jewish life gradually and sincerely.

Formal religious milestones should not be rushed before life is stable.

### 5. Creating Friendships

Beyond networking, the aim is to form real friendships and social roots.

Belonging matters more than efficiency or scale.

The right question is not only “who is useful to meet?” but “who makes life here feel real?”

### 6. Understanding Israel Professionally

Work is allowed to emerge, but should not dominate year one.

During the first year:

- Listen.
- Observe.
- Meet people without extracting value.
- Understand Israeli tech, creative, and business culture.
- Let ideas grow out of relationships and insight.

Do not force a new company or professional identity too early.

### 7. Financial Structure and Integration

Financial planning matters, but should be sequenced.

The year-one approach should be:

- Avoid accidental UK tax residence.
- Establish Israeli residence robustly.
- Understand reporting obligations.
- Review asset exposure gradually.
- Avoid fragile or premature structures.
- Use advisors for final decisions.

The goal is robustness, not cleverness.

### 8. Maintaining Existing Commitments

Simon may continue supporting existing commitments, including DELLI, in a limited and non-executive way.

These commitments should not dominate the first year or undermine the move.

UK and Italy travel should be planned carefully around tax-residence and day-count constraints.

---

## 7. Work and Business Posture

Work should be allowed to emerge from life in Israel.

Do not turn the first year into a performance sprint.

A good year-one professional posture is:

- Curious.
- Open.
- Available.
- Low pressure.
- Relationship-led.
- Observational before operational.

Businesses should grow out of real insight and real relationships, not urgency.

---

## 8. Judaism and Community

Jewish life is to be lived as experience, not managed as a project.

The priority is:

- Presence.
- Learning.
- Participation.
- Community.
- Sincerity.

Conversion, if relevant later, is a direction, not a deadline.

No formal conversion process should be forced before life is stable.

---

## 9. Advisors and Roles

Each advisor should have a clear role.

Avoid duplication, role confusion, and over-advising.

Typical advisor categories:

- Israeli lawyers and tax advisors: Israeli law, Aliyah, Israeli tax, company/structuring, lease and local legal issues.
- UK/Italian lawyers and accountants: UK and Italian tax, residence, IHT, domicile, treaty issues, and cross-border planning.
- Wealth / portfolio advisors: investment custody, portfolio structure, asset exposure, and long-term wealth planning.
- Practical support: logistics, documents, appointments, travel, household setup.

The goal is coordinated advice, not more advice.

---

## 10. Practical Operating Rules

For year one:

- Keep records carefully.
- Track UK days precisely.
- Keep travel plans conservative.
- Separate UK tax-residence questions from Israeli tax-residence questions.
- Do not make major structural decisions without advisor confirmation.
- Avoid signing or committing under pressure where possible.
- Review open questions regularly.
- Prefer simple, reversible steps.

---

## 11. Red Flags

Be cautious if a decision:

- Creates pressure for Chiara.
- Makes the first year feel like a tax project.
- Adds complexity before the basic move is stable.
- Depends on fragile assumptions.
- Requires perfect execution across multiple countries.
- Creates unnecessary UK presence.
- Forces business creation before belonging and routines are established.
- Cannot be reversed easily.

---

## 12. Year-One Success Definition

The first year is successful if Simon and Chiara:

- Feel physically and emotionally settled.
- Have a stable home in Israel.
- Build basic Hebrew confidence.
- Develop real friendships and community.
- Maintain a robust UK non-residence position.
- Establish a credible Israeli residence position.
- Understand the main tax/legal risks without being ruled by them.
- Keep optionality for the future.
- Avoid unnecessary complexity.

The first year is about laying foundations, not maximising output.

Any professional, financial, or structural activity should adapt to these priorities, not compete with them.
